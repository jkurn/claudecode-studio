The Two Tracks: Capability and Process
Every level of GTM autonomy requires progress on two fundamentally different tracks.
Conflating them is the fastest way to stall out. Capability is the raw technical infrastructure that
makes work possible. Process is the encoded knowledge of how to do the work well. One is
built by product teams and system integrators. The other is owned entirely by the sales
organization.

Capability: The Systems That Enable Work
Capability refers to every system a sales organization uses to get work done. The AI model
itself is a capability. The agent harness that orchestrates multi-step tasks is a capability. CRM
access, data enrichment tools, email integration, calendar connectivity, document generation —
these are all capabilities. They are not exclusively AI, but they form the infrastructure layer that
determines what is technically possible at any given moment.
Capability is driven by product development, operations teams, system administrators, and data
engineers. It is largely outside the control of an individual sales rep. When a new system comes
online — a new CRM connection, a new AI model, a new multi-agent framework — that is a
capability unlock. The rep did not create it, but they are now responsible for using it.
Key Distinction
Capability answers the question: "What can the system do?" It is the technical ceiling. When
capability expands, it does not mean value is automatically captured. It means the potential
for value has expanded. Realizing that potential is the job of process.

Process: The Knowledge That Directs Work
Process is how work gets done. It is the accumulated knowledge of what works, what doesn’t,
what matters in a specific deal, what questions to ask a specific persona, how to position
against a specific competitor, when to push and when to pull back. Process is the difference
between two sales reps in the same company, selling the same product, with access to the
same tools, yet producing wildly different results.
Process is akin to ramping up a new sales rep. They bring broad industry understanding, but
they need to learn the specific: the specific product, the specific ICP, the specific objections, the
specific competitive landscape, the specific way this company closes deals. These are encoded
preferences, curated from careers in sales, from trial and error, from the institutional knowledge
that lives in the heads of the best performers and rarely makes it into documentation.
Process answers the question: "How should the system do it?" It governs many scenarios rather
than a one-to-one situation, and that is precisely what makes it hard. A single capability like

Why This Distinction Matters
The process track always becomes the bottleneck when new capability comes online.
Connecting a CRM is one capability. But the amount of sales activity that touches a CRM is
orders of magnitude greater than the connection itself. Adding multi-agent systems is a single
capability. It unlocks dozens of sales activities that can now be performed autonomously. Each
one needs process.
This asymmetry is the central challenge of moving from being assisted by a single AI to
orchestrating a fleet of agents. Capability expands in steps. Process must expand to fill every
gap capability creates.

The Rings of Ownership: What You Control
A sales organization operates across four concentric rings of influence. Understanding where
you sit determines what you can practically change.
Ring

Description

Ring 1: Personal

Everything directly in your control — your preferences, selling style,
relationships, how you approach problems, position, pitch, negotiate.
Your process is yours to define.

Ring 2: Deal Team

The people who work with you to close a specific deal — your
manager, solutions engineer, CSM, account manager. You represent
and coordinate this team. Their work impacts your outcome, and yours
impacts theirs.

Ring 3: Sales Team

Your segment, office, region, or patch. The wider team that shares
your market and methodology. How they go to market shapes the
environment you sell in.

Ring 4: Sales Organization

The entire go-to-market function. Operates at a metrics, performance,
and forecasting level. A fundamentally different altitude than a quarterdriven individual rep.

This guide focuses on Ring 1 and Ring 2. These are where an individual sales rep can directly
affect change. Ring 3 and Ring 4 require separate organizational discussions. The crucial
insight is this: when told to “think like a VP of Sales,” most reps freeze because they associate
that with Ring 4 responsibilities. But a VP of Sales also operates at Ring 1 and Ring 2. The goal
is not to influence the whole organization — it is to apply leadership thinking to the
scope you already own.

Your Responsibility as a Rep
As a sales rep, you are ultimately responsible for your pipeline, your targets, and the
relationship with the customer. You coordinate the work that needs to happen for the customer
across your deal team. Not every input is something you do yourself, but you own the
coordination. Sales is the systematic process of uncovering hidden truths — asking the right
questions at the right time, following threads and hints to dig into the ultimate motivation behind
why a customer is evaluating your product. That is your job, and it is a fundamentally different
responsibility than what a sales manager, SE, or CSM carries. Being clear about this boundary
is what allows you to influence the work of others without overstepping, because you have done
your job well.

The Shift: From Individual Contributor to Division
Head
Here is the practical reality of AI in sales: the old adage of “more pipeline requires more
headcount” is beginning to decouple. But for that to truly take effect, a single rep needs to do
significantly more. The capability is arriving. The experience and process of wielding that
capability has not caught up.
A single sales rep who orchestrates a number of AI agents to perform a wider range of
customer interactions is, in practice, operating more like a division than an individual. What
does a head of sales think about? Top of funnel, middle of funnel, bottom of funnel. How to
ramp up activity and productivity. How to review and create metrics. How to forecast. How to
identify the separation of responsibilities across staff. How to assess where bottlenecks exist in
both process and capability.
These are all direct parallels to what an individual rep should start thinking about as they take
on a higher abstraction of sales responsibilities through AI.
The Mindset Gap
The vast majority of sales reps still operate with a one-to-one mindset: I have one target
and I directly control it. That mental model needs to evolve. A rep managing agents needs
to deconstruct their target — understand where pipeline sources come from, where
conversion bottlenecks exist, and how to allocate effort across the funnel. This does not
mean becoming a VP overnight. It means progressively applying that thinking at each level
of autonomy, starting with what you already control.

Using Your System to Learn From Your System
One of the most powerful and underappreciated aspects of this shift is the recursive learning
loop. You can use AI to learn about AI. You can use the system you are building to teach
yourself the competencies you will need next.
You do not need to wait for an expert to teach you forecasting best practices. You can bootstrap
a personal forecast using your own deal data and your AI, just for yourself, right now. When the
capability arrives to run forecasting across all your agents, you will have already experienced
what it is like to think that way. You will have built the mental model before the infrastructure
demanded it.
This is already happening in software engineering. Developers use AI coding tools to learn
patterns they have never implemented before, in real time, on real projects. The same dynamic
is now available to sales organizations. The barrier to building new competencies has collapsed.
The only barrier that remains is the willingness to start.

Level 1: Assisted — The Delegation Mindset
"I can break my work into tasks and hand them to someone who can help."

The Mindset Shift
At Level 1, the fundamental change is learning to decompose your own work. Most reps think in
outcomes: “I need to close this deal.” At L1, you need to start thinking in tasks: “I need to
research this account, then draft a follow-up, then update CRM, then prep for my call at 2 PM.”
That decomposition is the minimum cognitive shift.
This is deceptively simple. The reason L1 is still a real shift is that most people have never
needed to articulate their work in discrete, delegatable units. You just did it. Now you need to
explain it clearly enough that an AI can execute it. That exercise — making your implicit process
explicit, even temporarily — is the seed of everything that follows.

Process Requirements
At L1, the rep is still the entire brain. The AI is faster hands. Process lives entirely in the rep’s
head — they know what to ask for because they have built that intuition over years. The quality
of the output is directly proportional to the quality of the input.
This creates an important asymmetry: an experienced rep gets massive value from L1
because they know exactly what to delegate. A rep in their first 90 days may not even know
what questions to ask. This is the first place where the recursive learning loop matters — a new
rep can ask the AI itself: “What should I research before a first meeting with a mid-market SaaS
company?” and start building their process with the tool’s help.

Practical Examples: Ring 1 (Personal)
Top of Funnel
“Research this account before my cold outreach.” The rep identifies the target. The AI pulls
company data, recent news, key hires, technographic signals, and competitive landscape. The
rep uses that research to decide the angle of their outreach and writes the email with that
context. The judgment of which account, which angle, and what tone is entirely the rep’s. The
research legwork is the AI’s.

Middle of Funnel
“Prep me for my discovery call at 2 PM.” The AI pulls CRM data on the deal, summarizes prior
interactions, identifies the attendees and their roles, surfaces relevant case studies, and
suggests discovery questions based on the prospect’s industry. The rep walks into the call with
a brief they would have spent 30 minutes building manually.

Bottom of Funnel
“Draft a follow-up email summarizing what we agreed in yesterday’s negotiation call.” The AI
structures the email around the key commitments, next steps, and open items. The rep reviews,
adjusts tone for the specific relationship, and sends.

Practical Examples: Ring 2 (Deal Team)
At L1, the deal team impact is secondary but real. The rep can now share higher-quality
preparation with their SE or manager because the cost of producing it dropped dramatically.
Instead of walking into a deal review with notes in your head, you walk in with a written brief the
AI helped you assemble. Instead of verbally describing the deal status, you have a structured
summary. The deal team gets better inputs, which improves their contributions.

Level 2: Copilot — The Codification Mindset
"I know my own process well enough to write it down, and I’m willing to let the system run it."

The Mindset Shift
L2 is the first real mindset wall. The shift is from doing to describing. At L1, you told the AI what
to do each time. At L2, you are formalizing how you do things into repeatable sequences that
the AI executes end-to-end with your review.
This is harder than it sounds. Most reps have never codified their own process. They just do it.
Asking someone to write a playbook is asking them to be self-aware about their own workflow in
a way that selling has never required. It is the difference between being a practitioner and being
a teacher of your own practice.
This is where the recursive loop becomes directly practical. A rep can ask their AI: “Based
on the last ten discovery calls I prepped for with you, what is the pattern? What do I always ask
you to do? What steps do I always follow?” The system can help you extract your own process
from your own behavior. You do not have to start with a blank page.

Process Requirements
This is where process becomes the bottleneck for the first time. Each playbook is a process
decision. It encodes not just “do these five steps” but “do these five steps this way, with these
judgment calls baked in.” The rep needs to answer questions they may have never explicitly
considered:
What do I always do when prepping for a discovery call? What information do I always want?
What format do I want it in? What do I check first in CRM? How do I structure my follow-up

The Right Question to Ask Yourself
For each stage of your funnel, ask: "What do I do every single time, with almost no
variation?" That is your first playbook candidate. The answer identifies the highest-leverage,
lowest-risk processes to codify first.

Practical Examples: Ring 1 (Personal)
Top of Funnel
A “Prospect Research Playbook” that runs every time you add a new target account: pull
company data, check for existing CRM records, enrich key contacts, identify recent trigger
events (new hires, funding rounds, product launches), score against your ICP criteria, and
produce a one-page research brief in your preferred format. You initiate it by providing an
account name. The playbook runs end-to-end. You review the brief and decide whether to
pursue.

Middle of Funnel
A “Discovery Prep Playbook” that runs before every discovery call: pull all CRM data on the
deal, summarize prior touchpoints, research each attendee’s background and role, surface
relevant case studies by industry and use case, generate tailored discovery questions based on
what you know so far, and compile everything into a pre-call brief. You still control the
conversation. But the preparation is systematized.

Bottom of Funnel
A “Deal Review Playbook” you run weekly on each active opportunity: pull current deal data,
compare to last week’s status, identify risks and gaps in your deal qualification (e.g., missing
decision criteria, unclear timeline, unvalidated champion), and produce a structured summary
with recommended next actions. This is you performing your own pipeline review — applying
the “division head” thinking at the personal level.

Practical Examples: Ring 2 (Deal Team)
At L2, the deal team ring becomes genuinely actionable. A playbook can encode the handoff
between you and your SE: “When a deal reaches the demo stage, generate an SE brief
containing the prospect’s pain points, technical requirements, current stack, and specific
questions they’ve raised.” You are not just improving your own efficiency — you are improving
the interface between you and your deal team by ensuring they receive consistently highquality, structured inputs.

Level 3: Autopilot — The Management Mindset
"I define what good looks like, set the triggers, and manage exceptions rather than execution."

The Mindset Shift
L3 is where the rep genuinely becomes a manager of agents. Work happens without your
initiation. Playbooks that you built at L2 are now wired to signals — a deal stage change, an
inbound lead, a calendar trigger, an email going unanswered for 48 hours. The agents run. You
handle exceptions.
The cognitive shift here is from reviewing all work to defining standards and monitoring
deviations. You cannot read every email your agents draft. You cannot review every research
brief. Instead, you need to ask: what are the signals that tell me something went wrong? What
should trigger an escalation to me? What does “good enough” look like when I am not looking at
every output?
This is the same judgment a manager exercises when deciding which reps can run deals
independently and which need coaching on every call. You are now making that judgment
about your agents, mapped to your funnel.

The process requirements at L3 jump significantly because you are now encoding exception

instinct is the hardest thing to capture. At L3, you need to make it explicit:

For each automated playbook, you need to define: What triggers it? (A deal moving to a

output look like when it is working well? (A research brief that covers all key areas, a followup email that is on-tone and accurate.) What should cause an escalation? (A prospect asking

The Cost-of-Error Framework
For each stage of your funnel, ask: "What is the cost of an error at this stage?" At the top of
funnel, a mediocre cold email costs you one prospect’s attention. In the middle, a poorly
prepped demo could lose a deal. At the bottom, a mishandled negotiation email could cost
you revenue. Map your autonomy levels to this cost gradient: more autonomy where the
cost of error is low, tighter controls where it is high.

Practical Examples: Ring 1 (Personal)
Top of Funnel
A prospecting agent runs continuously: when new companies matching your ICP criteria appear
in your enrichment tools, the agent automatically researches them, scores them, and either
adds them to your prospecting queue with a brief or, for high-scoring accounts, drafts initial
outreach for your review. You are not initiating the research. You are reviewing the output of a
machine that is always scanning for opportunities.

Middle of Funnel
An active deal monitoring agent watches your pipeline. When a deal has had no activity for a
defined period, it drafts a re-engagement email. When a prospect replies to an email with
specific keywords (pricing, timeline, competitors), it triggers a deeper research pass and flags
the deal for your attention with context. When a discovery call is scheduled, it automatically runs
your Discovery Prep Playbook and puts the brief in your calendar event 30 minutes before the
call.

Bottom of Funnel
A deal progression agent monitors deals in late stages. When a proposal is sent and no
response comes within 48 hours, it drafts a check-in. When a champion goes quiet during the
procurement process, it surfaces the risk with suggested next actions. You still make every
decision at this stage. But the agent ensures nothing falls through the cracks and that you
always have a suggested next move.

Level 4: Self-Driving — The Strategic Mindset
"I set direction, define what success looks like, and the system figures out the tactics."

The Mindset Shift
At L4, the rep operates as a strategist. You are no longer defining individual playbooks or
reviewing individual outputs. You are setting direction: which market segments to prioritize, what
qualification bar to hold, what the target pipeline mix should look like, where to invest your
personal time versus delegate to agents.
The system at L4 observes what works across all the levels below it. It identifies patterns you
may not see: which outreach sequences convert best for which personas, which deal patterns
correlate with wins versus losses, where your funnel consistently leaks, what the optimal timing
is for follow-ups based on your actual data.
This is where the meta-thinking you’ve been building becomes essential. You are using the
system to learn from the system. The agents have generated more data and completed more
tasks than you could ever consume in detail. Your job is to ask the right questions: What is
working? What is not? Where should I shift resources? What should I stop doing? This is the
same altitude at which a head of division operates — but scoped to your Ring 1 and Ring 2.

The Ramp Is Not a Cliff
The progression from L1 to L4 is not a series of dramatic leaps. Each level inherits the work
from the level below. A good L1 interaction becomes the seed of an L2 playbook. A good L2
playbook becomes the foundation of an L3 automation. L3 performance data feeds L4
optimization. The flywheel connects every level.

The Capability Track Scales Down in Effort
Once the infrastructure is built, each incremental capability is cheaper to add. A new CRM
connection is a one-time setup. A new model upgrade applies everywhere. The capability effort
front-loads and then diminishes. This is why capability is often the easier track — it is finite and
measurable.

The Process Track Scales Up in Cognitive Demand
Process moves in the opposite direction. At L1, the process overhead is minimal: know what to
delegate. At L2, you must formalize your workflows. At L3, you must define quality standards
and exception handling. At L4, you must set strategic direction and evaluate systemic
performance. Each level asks you to think at a higher altitude.
But the ramp is different for everyone, and it does not need to happen all at once. The critical
insight is that you can start preparing for the next level before the capability demands it. Use
your AI to bootstrap a personal forecast before you need to forecast across agents. Use it to
review your own conversion patterns before the system can do it automatically. Every time you
use the tool to think about your own process, you are building the cognitive muscle for the next
level.
Start Where You Are
You do not need to think like a VP of Sales on day one. But at every level, there is a version
of that thinking that applies to your scope. At L1, it is as simple as: which tasks should I
delegate first? At L2: what does my process actually look like? At L3: what does good look
like without me watching? At L4: what should I be optimizing for? Each question is a small
step in the same direction.

What Stays Constant
Across every level, the rep’s core job does not change. You are still the person responsible for
the relationship with the customer. You are still the one who asks the right questions in a
discovery call, who reads the room in a negotiation, who builds trust over time. AI does not
replace that judgment. It amplifies it by removing the work that was never the highest use of
your time.
The capability will continue to expand. New models will come online. New integrations will be
connected. New agent frameworks will emerge. Much of that is outside your control. But what is
always in your control is how you think about your work, how you structure your process, and
how willing you are to let the system help you. Act as if the capability is already there,
because when it catches up, things will move very quickly for those who have already
built the mindset.
